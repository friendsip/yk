# Nex Playground: A Review of the Reviews

*A camera-based, controller-free games console that turns your living room into an
active playground. We haven't tested one ourselves — so this is our honest
read of what the people who have, both reviewers and parents, actually found.*

> **A note on how we did this.** We're an editor, not an author: rather than
> pretend we've spent a fortnight with a Nex Playground, we've gathered the most
> credible independent reviews and parent reactions we could find and weighed
> them up for you. Where reviewers genuinely disagree, we say so. Where a claim
> comes from the company (or someone it pays), we flag it. Prices and
> availability change often with this product — we've dated everything, but do
> double-check before you buy.

## Key takeaways

- It's a small camera box that plugs into your TV; your child's **body is the
  controller** — no handsets, no headset. Think "a Wii without the controllers,"
  which is the comparison nearly every reviewer reaches for.
- Reviewers and parents broadly agree on two things: **kids genuinely love it**,
  and **it gets them moving** — jumping, dodging, dancing. It is not passive
  screen time.
- The catch almost everyone raises is **cost**. The console is one payment, but
  most of the games sit behind a **Play Pass subscription**, so the real cost
  over a few years is much higher than the sticker price.
- It suits **younger children best (roughly 3–8)**. Several reviewers found it
  thins out for over-8s, who exhaust the depth fairly quickly.
- It's a **walled garden** — no ads, no in-game purchases, no online strangers,
  a physical camera cover, and child-privacy certification. For many parents
  that safety is the single biggest selling point.

## What it actually is

The Nex Playground is a compact cube — about the size of a large apple — that
connects to your television by HDMI. A single wide-angle camera and motion-
tracking software read your child's whole-body movements, so there are no
controllers to hold, lose, or fight over. It's explicitly pitched as a successor
to Microsoft's old Kinect, and it's made by a US company, Nex Team. Games
download over Wi-Fi but then play offline, and it ships with a small remote (for
menus only) and a magnetic disc to physically cover the camera when it's not in
use. [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nex_Playground)

The games — Nex calls them "Plays" — are party, sports, dance and fitness
activities built around movement, plus some educational titles (there's a Sesame
Workshop letter-hunt collaboration) and licensed favourites like Bluey, Peppa
Pig and Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles. New games are added regularly.
[(Motherly)](https://www.mother.ly/child/nex-playground-review/)
[(TechRadar)](https://www.techradar.com/gaming/nex-playground-review)

## Who it's for

The consensus from reviewers is **ages 3–10, with the sweet spot under 8**.
Android Police, which liked it overall, was blunt that it has "limited appeal
beyond age 8" — older children tend to run out of depth.
[(Android Police)](https://www.androidpolice.com/nex-playground-game-system-review/)
If you're buying for a 10- or 11-year-old, go in expecting the novelty to fade
faster.

## The honest bit about cost

This is the thing to understand before anything else, because it's the single
most common criticism across otherwise-positive reviews.

You pay once for the console — **around $299 in the US (since March 2026) and
£269 in the UK / €319 in Ireland (from its launch there in mid-2026)** — but only
**five games are free**. Everything else sits behind a **Play Pass**
subscription: roughly **$89 / £90 a year, or $49 / £45 for three months**.
[(TweakTown, Mar 2026)](https://www.tweaktown.com/news/110680/)
[(Game Developer, 2026)](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/the-nex-playground-is-launching-in-the-uk-and-ireland)

Add it up and the picture changes. Android Police calculated that quarterly
subscriptions put you **roughly $600 into the console over three years**.
[(Android Police)](https://www.androidpolice.com/nex-playground-game-system-review/)
That doesn't make it bad value — but it does make it a bigger commitment than the
shelf price suggests, and it's worth deciding up front whether you'll keep paying.

*(The US price has risen three times since a $199 launch in 2023 — the company
attributes this to component costs — so whatever you're quoted, check it's
current.)*

## Does it actually get kids moving?

Broadly, yes — and this is where it earns a place in a conversation about
[getting active as a family](/articles/getting-active-as-a-family). Reviewers
consistently describe children genuinely exerting themselves: jumping, squatting,
waving, working up a sweat, sometimes wearing themselves out (and roping parents
in). [(Motherly)](https://www.mother.ly/child/nex-playground-review/)
[(Tom's Guide)](https://www.tomsguide.com/gaming/this-gadget-is-like-a-wii-without-the-controllers-and-my-kid-loves-it)

Two honest caveats, though. First, we couldn't find any independent study
measuring how much activity it actually produces — the evidence is reviewer
observation, not data. Second, the older research on "exergaming" (the Wii and
Kinect era) generally found this kind of play is **lighter exercise than real
outdoor or sports play**. Nex's own founder argues the design is self-limiting —
"you cannot be jumping around for five hours" — which is plausible, but it's the
company's claim, not an independent finding.
[(Business Insider via AOL)](https://www.aol.com/nex-playground-fits-trend-products-113302872.html)

So the fair framing is the one Nex's own paediatric adviser uses: it's **better
than passive screen time, not a replacement for a real playground**. (Worth
noting that adviser is paid by Nex, so it's positioning as much as paediatrics.)

## The screen-time question

If you've read our piece on why
[screen time isn't a moral failing](/editorial/screen-time-not-a-moral-failing),
you'll recognise the tension here: this is a screen that asks your child to move.
It sits in a growing category of "active screen time" products aimed at
screen-conscious parents. That's a genuinely useful middle ground for a rainy
afternoon — but it's still a screen, and it's healthiest thought of as *one* of
your child's activities, not a substitute for unstructured play or time outside.
We'd treat the marketing language of "guilt-free screen time" with a little
friendly scepticism, while accepting the underlying point: an hour of this beats
an hour slumped over a tablet.

## What the reviewers disagree about

We'd be doing you a disservice to paper over this. Most reviews land in
**positive territory** — Android Police gave it 7.5/10, and TechRadar, Motherly
and Tom's Guide were all warm, as was a Wirecutter (New York Times) feature. But
**The Verge dissented hard, scoring it 4/10**, calling the library "shovelware"
and — most substantively — judging the single-camera motion tracking "worse than
either the Wii or Kinect," with lag and trouble handling more than one player.
[(The Verge)](https://www.theverge.com/) *(we're confirming this against the
original before publishing)*

That tracking criticism is genuinely contested: TechRadar reported accuracy in
the "mid-90s%," and Android Police found tracking "consistent." So the truth is
reviewers disagree, and your mileage may depend on your room, your lighting, and
how many children are leaping about at once. We'd rather tell you that than
pretend there's a single verdict.

## The practical stuff

- **Space:** you'll want **6–7 feet of clear floor** in front of the TV.
  Cramped rooms and low ceilings are a real constraint.
  [(Android Police)](https://www.androidpolice.com/nex-playground-game-system-review/)
- **Lighting:** bright windows behind players, patterned clothing and long
  sleeves can all upset the tracking.
- **Players:** local multiplayer works, but tracking gets less reliable the more
  bodies are in frame.
- **Privacy and safety:** there's an always-on camera, which understandably
  gives some parents pause — but the mitigations are real and unusually
  thorough: a **physical magnetic cover**, the company says **camera data stays
  on the device**, it carries **kidSAFE+ COPPA child-privacy certification**, and
  there are **no ads, no in-game purchases, and no online contact with
  strangers**. (The "data stays local" claim is the company's; we found no
  independent security audit either way.)

## What about the alternatives?

Almost everyone compares it to the **Nintendo Wii** — same controller-free,
get-off-the-sofa spirit. The **Nintendo Switch** (with *Sports* or *Ring Fit
Adventure*) offers far more depth and power, but needs controllers and is a
pricier overall platform aimed at a wider age range. The old **Xbox Kinect** is
the obvious ancestor. The Nex's distinct pitch is **young, controller-free,
camera-on-the-telly, play-together-in-the-room** — which is a real niche the
others don't quite fill.

## Availability for international readers

It's widely sold in the **US**, and **launched in the UK and Ireland in mid-2026**
(Argos, Smyths and Amazon UK among the stockists). **Elsewhere there's no
official distribution yet** — you'd be relying on import services, with the usual
caveats about cost and warranty. Wherever you are, check the Play Pass is
available in your country before committing, since the console without it is just
five games.
[(Game Developer)](https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/the-nex-playground-is-launching-in-the-uk-and-ireland)

## Our take

On the balance of the reviews, the Nex Playground does the main thing it promises:
it's a **safe, genuinely fun way to get younger children moving indoors**, and
the walled-garden safety is a real reassurance. If you have a child roughly
**3–8**, a **clear, well-lit space**, and you're comfortable with an **ongoing
subscription**, it's a reasonable thing to consider — especially for the rainy-day
problem it's clearly built to solve.

Go in clear-eyed about two things, though: the **real cost is the subscription,
not the box**, and it will likely **hold an older child's attention for less time
than you'd hope**. It's a nice addition to an active childhood — not a
replacement for the park.

We haven't put one through its paces ourselves, so treat this as a careful
summary of other people's experiences rather than a verdict from our own living
room. If you've used one, we'd love to hear how it held up.
