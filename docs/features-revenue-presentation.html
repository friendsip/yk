The landscape

SEO traffic is dying. Habit and email are the new assets.

~60%

of Google searches now end without a click; CTR falls to ~8% under AI Overviews. Review/affiliate content is hit hardest.

½ direct

Mumsnet's traffic declines monthly — but ~half of what remains is direct. The destination habit survives; drive-by SEO doesn't.

$32 / 1k

Family-category newsletters earn $32 per 1,000 subscribers per sponsored send — the top of all consumer categories (Paved, 2026).

What this means for us

  • Generic SEO articles alone are the dying model. Tools, age-personalisation, email programmes and a trusted voice convert drive-by visits into direct habit.
  • The weekly-batch editorial engine is the content supply; the question is what wraps around it to create return visits and a list.

Who's winning, and how

Four proof points worth copying from

Good Inside (Dr Becky)

$34M/yr, 100k members at ~$25/mo. An AI coach ("Gigi") grounded strictly in her own vetted corpus. The pattern: trusted voice + RAG chatbot, not generic GPT.

NYT Games

11.1bn plays in 2024; $10/mo Games-only family plan (Sept 2025). Subscribers who play + read retain far better. Families already co-play dailies — nobody has built the kids-first daily.

Twinkl

£98.9M revenue FY2025 from a printables subscription — teachers, parents, nurseries. Proof that quality-assured family printables scale enormously.

Raising Children Network

The best no-login age-stage architecture: every page keyed to pregnancy→teen bands, development trackers as content pages. Closest structural model to us.

The games area

The roadmap's games bet is validated — with two upgrades

  • Families demonstrably pay for puzzle bundles: NYT launched a $10/mo Games-only family plan in Sept 2025 specifically so every family member gets their own streak; family plans drove a Q3 subscriber jump.
  • The kids/family daily-puzzle niche is nearly empty — dozens of adult dailies, almost no child-calibrated ones. PBS Kids (~40M plays/month) proves children's browser games at scale.
  • Upgrade 1 — go daily, not just sessional: add one daily co-play puzzle to the planned lineup. The daily appointment is the retention mechanic, and it's mostly a content-scheduling problem.
  • Upgrade 2 — family streaks, not child streaks: household-level, no loss-aversion penalties. Duolingo-style individual streaks for kids are now widely criticised; Prodigy Math (16 upsell ads in 19 minutes of play) is the anti-pattern that drew an FTC complaint.
Compliance is a design choice. No child accounts, no ads, no profiling, localStorage-only progress, parent-held subscription = minimal COPPA exposure (amended rule fully in force April 2026). The UK Children's Code still applies — a DPIA and high-privacy defaults are needed regardless. Our planned model is already close to the minimal-burden configuration.

Revenue — what the evidence favours

Four strategic conclusions

1 · The list is the asset

Family newsletters monetise at the top of all consumer categories. Display ads are traffic-fragile and stylistically wrong for a premium illustration-led site. Every feature should feed the email list.

2 · Utility membership, not persona membership

Every big parenting membership (Good Inside, ParentData, Big Little Feelings) is persona-led. Without a face, our membership case is utility: games + printables vault + Ask yourkids + age-keyed digest. The free-subscription games gate already builds this muscle.

3 · The best affiliate isn't toys

Family finance pays $20–75 per lead (life insurance, wills); edtech & boxes pay $10–20 bounties (Outschool 20%, KiwiCo, Lovevery). Baby gear via Amazon is 1–3%. "New parents need life insurance" content is the highest-EPC play — and it's genuinely useful advice.

4 · B2B is the sleeper

Twinkl (£99M) and Nemours KidsHealth prove institutions buy quality-assured family content (nurseries, employers' family-benefits platforms, health systems). Buyers demand editorial provenance — our human-reviewed curation model is an asset here, pure AI generation is disqualifying.

8

What to avoid

Evidence-backed "do not build" list

  • Child-facing AI chat — Character.AI banned under-18s from open-ended chat (Nov 2025) under lawsuits and FTC investigation. Parent-facing AI lands well; child-facing AI is legally and reputationally toxic.
  • Open anonymous forums — the dying acquisition channel (Mumsnet), a moderation money pit, and a Children's Code burden. Structured expert Q&A gets the community value without the cost.
  • Individual streaks / loss-aversion mechanics for children — ICO games guidance explicitly targets reward-mechanic nudges; the 2025 commentary on Duolingo-for-kids is uniformly negative.
  • Display ads now — below ~50k sessions it's pocket money, it fights the brand, and the sector revenue is falling. Revisit at Mediavine threshold, if ever.
  • Unreviewed AI-generated story/content products — parents accept AI images only with human-authored text and expert review; "AI slop" backlash is real and growing. Our Site Bible already forbids this; the market agrees with it.

The one-line summary

Build the daily habit (games + tools + printables), feed everything into the email list, and let membership, sponsorship and B2B licensing grow on top of it.

The existing roadmap is structurally right. The evidence says: lean harder into daily mechanics, age personalisation, and the list — and stay disciplined about the "do not build" list, which mostly matches guardrails we already have.
