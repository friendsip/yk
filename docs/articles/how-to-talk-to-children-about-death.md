# How to Talk to Children About Death

*Article | Health, Family Life*

---

There is almost no subject that adults find harder to discuss with children than death. The instinct is to protect — to soften, deflect, or delay. But the evidence from child development and bereavement research is consistent: children are better served by honest, age-appropriate conversations about death than by silence, euphemism, or information withheld until crisis forces disclosure.

This article draws on guidance from child bereavement specialists and developmental research to help you have these conversations — before, during, and after a bereavement.

---

## Why Avoidance Usually Makes Things Worse

Children are aware of death from very early on — through the death of pets, observed natural death, stories, films, and the conversations of adults around them. They form ideas about it whether or not adults engage with those ideas. Left to their own conclusions, they often imagine something worse than the truth, or arrive at explanations that carry unnecessary guilt or fear.

When adults avoid the subject, children learn that death is something too frightening or too painful for the grown-ups to discuss. This makes it harder for children to ask questions when they need to, and harder for them to process grief when a death occurs. The silence doesn't protect — it isolates.

---

## Talking About Death Before a Bereavement

The best time to introduce the concept of death, for most families, is outside the crisis of a bereavement — when there is space to be calm and curious together.

Common opportunities include:
- The death of a pet
- Finding a dead bird or animal outdoors
- A character dying in a book or film
- A child asking questions directly ("What happens when we die?" is a question most children ask at some point)

These moments can feel uncomfortable. Use them anyway. A child who has had gentle, honest conversations about death — that everything that lives eventually dies, that dying usually means a body stops working, that the people we love can feel sad and will miss them — is better equipped for a real bereavement than a child for whom it has never been mentioned.

## Language and Age

**Concrete language matters.** Euphemisms — "gone to sleep," "passed away," "we lost him," "went to a better place" — are well-intentioned but often confusing to children, and sometimes frightening. A child told that Grandpa "went to sleep" may develop a fear of sleep. A child told they "lost" someone may feel responsible for finding them.

Use the words: dead, died, death. They are not harmful. They are clear.

**Explanations should match developmental stage:**

- **Under 3:** Very young children have limited understanding of permanence. Keep it very simple: "Grandma died. That means we won't see her again." Expect the child to move on quickly — this is not callousness, it's developmental. Grief in very young children often appears and disappears in ways that can surprise adults.

- **Ages 3–5:** Children at this age often don't understand that death is permanent and universal. They may ask repeatedly when the person is coming back. Be patient and consistent: "When someone dies, they don't come back. Their body stopped working and we won't see them again." Magical thinking is common — watch for any beliefs that they caused the death.

- **Ages 5–8:** Children now understand permanence and may begin to think about the deaths of people they love, including parents. Questions about what happens to the body, and sometimes about what happens to the person (spiritually or otherwise), become more common. Answer honestly within your own family's belief framework.

- **Ages 9–12:** More sophisticated understanding. May want factual information, may want to understand causes, may begin to confront their own mortality. Don't shy away from real explanations. Reassure without making promises you can't keep.

- **Teenagers:** Can engage with death at an adult level. May need privacy to process as well as the knowledge that you are available. Be alert for complicated grief responses.

## When a Death Has Happened

### Tell the child yourself, as soon as possible
Children who find out about a death from someone other than a trusted adult — through overhearing a conversation, a text from a friend, or a teacher — often carry additional distress about how they found out. If possible, tell the child directly, in person, in a quiet space.

### Be honest about the cause
This includes difficult causes, including suicide. The research on this is counterintuitive but robust: children who are given honest, age-appropriate explanations of suicide (a person's brain was very, very unwell, so they ended their own life) cope better in the long term than children given vague or misleading explanations. Secrecy and confusion complicate grief.

### Expect different reactions
Children do not grieve in the way adults expect. They may appear distressed for a short time and then ask to go and play. They may cry at apparently unrelated moments. They may not cry at all. All of this is normal. Grief in children is often intermittent — they dip in and out of it — which can look like not caring, when it is simply how children protect themselves from being overwhelmed.

### Maintain routine where possible
Routine provides structure and safety. School, mealtimes, bedtimes — the ordinary architecture of daily life — can feel very important to a grieving child. Disrupting all routines at once can increase the sense of the world becoming unpredictable.

### Answer questions honestly
Children will ask questions that feel impossible to answer. "Why did she have to die?" "Will you die?" "Will I die?" These deserve honest, careful responses rather than deflection. It's okay to say you don't know. It's okay to say "I hope not, and I'm going to do everything I can to be here for you." What matters is that you engage.

## Signs a Child May Need More Support

Grief is not a disorder, and most children process bereavement without needing formal intervention. But watch for:

- Prolonged regression (returning to bedwetting, baby talk, or other earlier behaviours)
- Persistent refusal to return to school
- Significant changes in eating or sleeping that continue for months
- Withdrawal from friends and activities they previously enjoyed
- Expression of wanting to die or be with the person who died (take this seriously and seek advice)
- Persistent guilt or self-blame

If you're concerned, speak to your GP, school, or contact **Winston's Wish** (winstonswish.org), the UK's childhood bereavement charity, which has a helpline and extensive resources for families.

---

## For Families With Religious or Spiritual Beliefs

Beliefs about what happens after death are deeply personal, and there is no universal answer. What research suggests is that a coherent framework — whatever its content — is generally more helpful for children than uncertainty or inconsistency. If your family has beliefs about what follows death, sharing them honestly and as beliefs ("we believe...") rather than facts can provide comfort while also modelling appropriate epistemic humility.
