There are dozens of apps, services, and devices marketed to parents as solutions to the problem of children's online safety. Some are genuinely useful. Many are not — or at least, not in the ways they claim to be. And none of them replace the conversations that remain the most effective long-term safety tool available.

This guide focuses specifically on what works, what doesn't, and what to prioritise at different ages — with a focus on tools and services available to UK families.

---

## What Online Safety Tools Can and Can't Do

Before looking at specific tools, it's worth being clear about their limitations.

**What they can do:**
- Filter out a large proportion of harmful content, particularly for younger children
- Set limits on usage time and the apps children can access
- Give parents visibility into what children are doing online
- Block access to specific websites or categories of content
- Set quiet hours (e.g. no devices after 9pm)

**What they can't do:**
- Catch everything. Filters are imperfect and teenagers who want to circumvent them usually can.
- Replace trust and conversation. A child who has been given no framework for thinking about online risk is not safer for having parental controls — they are simply less prepared for the moment the controls fail or are removed.
- Protect older teenagers reliably. The more sophisticated the user, the less effective most filtering tools are.

The implication is not that tools are useless — they are genuinely helpful, particularly for younger children. It's that they are one layer of protection, not a complete strategy.

## Router-Level Controls

The most comprehensive approach for primary-age children is filtering at router level — this means that any device connecting to your home network, regardless of app or browser, has the same protections applied.

**BT, Sky, Virgin, TalkTalk, and EE** all offer free parental controls that can be activated through your account. These use DNS filtering to block categories of content. They are not foolproof — they can be bypassed by a mobile data connection — but they are a good baseline for younger children.

**Circle (meetcircle.com)** is a paid hardware device that plugs into your router and gives more granular control: per-child profiles, per-app time limits, and the ability to pause the internet entirely. It also covers mobile devices through an app. It costs around £100 plus a small monthly subscription. Worth considering if you want more detailed control without managing everything through individual device settings.

## Device-Level Controls

For school-age children with their own devices, these are the most common options:

### iOS (iPhone/iPad)
Apple's built-in **Screen Time** (Settings > Screen Time) is more capable than it is often given credit for. It allows:
- Content restrictions (film age ratings, explicit music, adult websites)
- Communication limits (who the child can contact)
- App restrictions (which apps can be used and when)
- Downtime schedules (devices off during sleep hours)
- Screen time reports

Set a Screen Time passcode that your child doesn't know. Without this, the controls are easily removed.

### Android
**Google Family Link** provides similar functionality for Android devices and allows parental oversight through a linked parent account. It can also track your child's location if needed.

### Chromebook
If your child uses a school Chromebook at home, controls may already be applied by the school. For home Chromebooks, Google Family Link also covers these.

## Social Media-Specific Considerations

Most social media platforms are officially restricted to 13+ (under GDPR requirements), but age verification is minimal. The Online Safety Act 2023 is gradually introducing tougher requirements on platforms, including age assurance for adult content sites — but implementation is ongoing.

In the meantime:

**Instagram:** Parental supervision tools are available for users under 18. Go to Settings > Supervision. This allows you to see what accounts your child follows, set usage time limits, and receive alerts.

**YouTube:** For younger children, use **YouTube Kids** rather than the main YouTube app — the content is filtered and more age-appropriate. For older children, Restricted Mode (available in YouTube settings) reduces but doesn't eliminate inappropriate content. Filtering YouTube reliably is genuinely hard; conversation about what they're watching and why is more effective than technical controls.

**TikTok:** TikTok's Family Pairing feature (available in Settings > Digital Wellbeing > Family Pairing) links a parent account to a child account, allowing screen time limits, content filtering, and DM controls.

**Snapchat:** Family Centre (available in Snapchat settings) allows parents to see who their child is communicating with (though not the content of messages) and to report accounts. Available for users under 18.

## Monitoring vs. Surveillance: Getting the Balance Right

There is an important distinction between age-appropriate oversight and covert surveillance — and getting this wrong, particularly with teenagers, tends to backfire.

Monitoring tools that children and teenagers know about and have discussed maintain trust while still providing a safety net. Covert surveillance tends to be discovered, and when it is, it damages the relationship that is your most important long-term safety tool.

The NSPCC and Internet Matters both recommend what they call "open monitoring": be transparent that you can see certain things (who they contact, what apps they use), while making clear that the goal is their safety, not punishing normal teenage behaviour.

For younger children, more comprehensive oversight is appropriate and expected. As children move through adolescence, the gradual extension of trust and privacy — with monitoring stepping back accordingly — tends to produce better outcomes than either constant surveillance or sudden removal of all controls.

## The Conversation Is the Core Tool

Every online safety organisation — the NSPCC, Internet Matters, the UK Safer Internet Centre — identifies the same thing as the most important protective factor: children who can talk to a trusted adult when something goes wrong online.

This means:
- Talking about online life regularly, not only when problems arise
- Reacting calmly when your child shares something concerning, rather than in ways that discourage future disclosure
- Making clear that if something upsets or frightens them online, they will not be in trouble for telling you
- Being curious about what they enjoy online, not just what might harm them

The tools in this article are useful scaffolding. The conversation is the foundation.
